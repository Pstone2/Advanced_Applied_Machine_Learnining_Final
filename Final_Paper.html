Neat

The genetic algorithm I chose to use for the project was NEAT. I had seen many videos of people using NEAT to play many different games, including Flappy Brid, so I knew it would work well. Specifically, I used the NEAT-Python library.

Neat’s Background

Neat was originally created by Ken Stanley during his time at the University of Texas. NEAT stands for the NeuroEvolution of Augmenting Topologies and is a great way to train a machine to do a task so long as there is a quantifiable goal for the be trained on.

Ideas Behind NEAT and Genetic Algorithms

The idea behind Genetic Algorithms and NEAT is rooted in the evolution of real-world animals. Charles Darwin’s theory of evolution tells us that over time animals will evolve based on selective pressures as only the most fit individuals will reproduce. Luckily for us, when we do this on a computer, we do not have to wait very long, and we have the ability to force the extinction of any versions that do not meet our fitness standards instantly.

What makes NEAT special

The part of NEAT that makes it so useful for training the birds is that it seeks to maintain a level of diversity amongst the population, even if it means preserving some individuals who are more unfit than others. Each generation, NEAT will take all of the different models and cross them with each other. Just like in the real world, this helps to preserve innovation and keep the modules from not being able to adapt to anything. Genetic algorithms that do not preserve more unfit generations will end up becoming very hyperfocused on whatever the first innovation amongst the population is. This is clearly bad as it can result in the machine coming to a non-optimal solution because it found a pretty good one. This is not to say that NEAT does not cut any unfit models, as the video below will show NEAT results in a population trend toward the best solution while ensuring the model is able to try different things as it goes along.

Bugs

Viewers should see the speed of the game does not reset unit the next generation passes the first pipe. At first, I was going to fix this, but I noticed the birds were learning to adapt to the fast speed at the beginning of the game. It is really interesting and speaks to the strength of NEAT for them to have all learned to fly upwards at the beginning as fast as possible.
